# Chapter 2 - Matthew Cuthbert is Surprised

Matthew Cuthbert enjoyed his five-mile drive to the Bright River station in peace. It was a pretty drive, winding through quiet valleys and past pleasant homesteads, with occasional glimpses of the blue gulf in the distance. The cherry trees in the orchards were white with bloom, and the air was sweet with the fragrance of clover.

When he reached Bright River there was no sign of any train. He tied his horse in the shelter of the small station yard and went inside. The platform was almost deserted; the only living creature in sight being a girl who was sitting on a pile of shingles at the far end.

Matthew, who was extremely shy around women and terrified of young girls, walked past her with his eyes on the ground. He entered the station office and inquired of the stationmaster when the train from Charlottetown was expected.

"The passenger train came and went half an hour ago," said the stationmaster. "But there was a passenger left for you—a little girl. She's sitting out there on the shingles now. I asked her to go into the waiting room, but she informed me she preferred to stay outside, as there was more scope for her imagination."

Matthew's heart sank. He stared at the stationmaster in blank dismay.

"There must be some mistake," he stammered. "I was expecting a boy. We sent word to the asylum to bring us a boy about eleven or twelve years old."

"Well, all I know is that she was brought by the lady on the train, who gave her into my charge and said you'd be along to collect her," replied the stationmaster cheerfully. "I don't have any boys in stock, Matthew. You'll have to make the best of what's out there on the shingles."

Matthew walked slowly out of the station, feeling like a man who had been asked to capture a wild beast. He approached the pile of shingles, where the girl sat waiting.

She was about eleven years old, dressed in a very short, very tight dress of yellowish-grey wincey. She had two thick braids of red hair hanging down her back, and her face was small, thin, and freckled. But her eyes were large, green, and full of a restless, brilliant spirit that seemed to look right through him.

As Matthew approached, she stood up, holding a small faded carpet bag in one hand.

"I suppose you are Mr. Matthew Cuthbert of Green Gables?" she said in a clear, sweet voice. "I was beginning to be afraid you weren't coming, and I was trying to decide what would be the most romantic place to spend the night if you didn't. I had settled on that big wild cherry tree at the corner of the road. I wouldn't have been a bit afraid, and it would have been lovely to sleep in a cherry tree in full bloom, don't you think?"

Matthew, completely overwhelmed by her speech and her extraordinary green eyes, found himself taking her hand and saying, "Well, now, I'm sorry I was late. Let's go get the horse."
